## PART 1: VOICE RULES

Each rule is grounded in census whitespace data.

### Rule 1: Write in calm, understated tone
**DATA**: 89.8% of direct competitor copy uses startup register. Only 1.7% of the entire dataset (10 of 589) uses calm energy. Understated personality is 12.1% overall but 0% among direct competitors in the startup register. The most memorable lines in the dataset — "Sharpen your thinking" (Obsidian), "Speak Freely" (Signal), "Free your mind" (Standard Notes) — all deploy calm energy. Calm is the structural whitespace.
**APPLICATION**: Every Capsule headline should read as if spoken at conversational volume. No exclamation marks. No breathless urgency. The product is confident enough to whisper.

### Rule 2: Lead with privacy as conviction, not checkbox
**DATA**: 0 of 10 direct competitor H1s mention privacy. Only 1.1% (3 of 274) of direct competitor copy elements have privacy as primary appeal. Direct competitors use "encrypted" zero times, "end-to-end" zero times. Privacy/security brands use specific technical language: "encrypted" (11x), "end-to-end" (7x). The gap between vague reassurance and privacy conviction is Capsule's core linguistic territory.
**APPLICATION**: Use privacy/security brand language in a transcription context. Say "your audio never leaves your device" — not "we take security seriously." Name the mechanism.

### Rule 3: Use contrast and negative framing
**DATA**: Contrast framing appears in 0.2% (1 of 589) of the entire dataset. Problem framing appears in 2.4% (14 of 589). No H1 in the dataset uses negative/contrast structure. Tactiq's "without an AI bot joining the meeting" is one of the strongest lines in the dataset and uses this exact structure.
**APPLICATION**: Define Capsule by what it does NOT do. "Never uploads." "No bots." "Zero bytes sent." Implicit contrast is more powerful than naming competitors — "never" implies others do.

### Rule 4: Use concrete numbers over vague promises
**DATA**: Only 1.1% (3 of 271) of headlines use the statistic pattern. The entire landscape deals in vague promises. "99% accuracy" (Sonix) and "4x faster" (WhisprFlow) are nearly alone. Number-led openings appear in 0.7% (2 of 271) of headlines.
**APPLICATION**: Wherever possible, substitute claims with data. "Cost+10%" instead of "affordable." "0 bytes uploaded" instead of "private." "€0.006/minute" instead of "fair pricing."

### Rule 5: Name the audience, do not generalise
**DATA**: 83.7% (493 of 589) of all copy is generic. Only 2.5% (15 of 589) is use-case specific. "Journalist," "researcher," and "ADHD" appear zero times across 589 elements. The strongest H1 in the direct competitor set — Granola's "The AI notepad for people in back-to-back meetings" — is one of the only segment-specific headlines.
**APPLICATION**: Name the people we serve: journalists who protect sources, researchers who own their data, professionals with ADHD who need reliable capture without cognitive overhead. Specificity signals understanding.

### Rule 6: Frame as outcome/identity, not feature list
**DATA**: 81.8% (482 of 589) of all copy uses feature framing. Benefit framing is 5.6%, outcome framing is 5.1%, identity framing is 4.9%. The "transcribe, summarize, search" feature trifecta appears across 5+ competitors. Feature-first framing is the single most saturated structural choice.
**APPLICATION**: Don't describe what Capsule does — describe what the user becomes or achieves. Not "transcribes audio locally" but "your words stay yours." Not "AI-powered notes" but "think clearly, capture everything."

### Rule 7: Explain the WHY behind "free"
**DATA**: "Free" is the most used power word (54 of 589 elements). 60% of brands offer a free tier. CTAs saturated with "free": "Get started free," "Try for free," "Free forever." But zero brands explain WHY something is free. "Free because your machine does the work" exists nowhere.
**APPLICATION**: Never just say "free." Always pair it with the structural reason: "Free because it runs on your device — no servers, no data centre, no reason to charge you."

### Rule 8: Deploy craft/editorial register
**DATA**: Craft appeal is 0.4% (1 of 274) among direct competitors. Editorial register is 3.1% (18 of 589) and 100% of that is Anthropic. The transcription category has zero craft positioning. Adjacent brands (Obsidian, Things 3, Linear) prove tools can carry a craft-forward voice.
**APPLICATION**: Write copy that feels considered, not generated. Short sentences. Intentional word choices. The page should read like it was written by someone who cares about language — because the product is for people who care about language.

## PART 4: CREATIVE DIRECTION SUMMARY

### The Central Thesis

Every transcription tool shouts the same thing in the same voice: "We're an AI meeting tool! We're fast! We're powerful! Trust us!" (startup register, bold personality, medium energy, feature framing, noun-led headlines).

Capsule enters the room quietly and says something nobody else is saying:

> "Your words never leave your device."

This is not a feature. It is a structural position. It cannot be copied by cloud-dependent competitors without rebuilding their entire product. It is said in a voice the category has never heard — calm, understated, considered.

### Three Creative Pillars

1. **Privacy as architecture, not afterthought** — Capsule's privacy story is structural (on-device processing), not a compliance checkbox. The copy must make this distinction visceral.

2. **Cost as honesty** — Capsule's cost+10% model is a transparency play that no competitor can match without exposing their own margins. The copy should make competitor markup visible.

3. **Quiet confidence** — In a landscape of shouting, silence is the most disruptive sound. Capsule's copy should feel like the product: dark glass, not bright bubbly corporate.
